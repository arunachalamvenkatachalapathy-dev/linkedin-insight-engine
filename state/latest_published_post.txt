Noticed a recurring blind spot in the Journal of Hazardous Materials regarding urban runoff management that we need to address immediately. We are still designing stormwater infrastructure as if PFAS and microplastics are isolated problems. They aren't. Research confirms that microplastics function as primary vectors, effectively extending the transport distance of fluorinated PFAS compounds throughout urban systems. 

My take? The current industry reliance on singular contaminant stream filtration is a structural failure. When these pollutants co-occur, they don't behave according to the standard models we used for traditional sediment basins. We are building systems that are fundamentally vulnerable to these synergistic transport effects. 

If we keep ignoring the co-occurrence, our tertiary filtration stages are destined for rapid, premature failure. It is not just a regulatory compliance issue; it is a design flaw. Practitioners need to stop treating these as separate streams during the planning phase. We have to account for the way these particles carry pollutants further than our current hydrodynamic separators ever predicted. How are you adjusting your site-specific design load to account for these multi-contaminant transport vectors in your latest stormwater project?
